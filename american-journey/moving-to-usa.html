Moving to the USA

Making the Decision

The decision for Adam and Emilie to emigrate from Germany grew out of several pressures. Their “mixed” marriage—Emilie from a Protestant family and Adam from a Catholic one—had created strain. Added to that were unsettled employment prospects in the late 1920s and the rise of the Nazi movement. Roger notes there were rumors that Adam was not in favor with the Nazis and may have had Communist affiliations, which, whether true or not, would have further complicated life in Germany.

“It took a great deal of courage to leave your birthplace and come to America. Not knowing anyone except the Ottos through the mail, not knowing the language, no idea as to the future. It must have been very difficult to leave behind their two-year-old—but as we all know, it was a terrific decision. Without their coming here, the entire family would have grown up in Germany and lived through the hardships of World War II.” — Egon Schreiner

The path to America began with an act of charity after the 1921 BASF explosion in Ludwigshafen. Through the Red Cross, Emilie or her mother received a donated fur coat from a family in the United States. In its pocket was a piece of paper with the donors’ name and address. Emilie saved that slip and later wrote to ask whether they would sponsor her and Adam to immigrate.

The correspondence timeline is unknown, but the donors—Mr. and Mrs. Paul Otto of Brooklyn (later on Long Island)—agreed to sponsor them. With arrangements made, Adam and Emilie sailed from Hamburg on June 20, 1930, and arrived in New York City on June 28, 1930. Because they were sponsored and met at the regular pier, they did not disembark at Ellis Island and did not go through the usual immigration processing there.

Given the uncertainties of starting over—new country, no job yet, and no clear prospects—Egon (then two years old) stayed behind in Oppau with Emilie’s parents until Adam and Emilie could establish themselves. Emilie later referred to a photograph with their sponsors, the Otto family, as her first picture in America.
